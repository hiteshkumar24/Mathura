The History of Mathura

Ancient Period

Mathura, situated on the banks of the Yamuna River in Uttar Pradesh, India, is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world. Its history dates back over 3,000 years, making it a site of great archaeological and cultural importance. According to Hindu mythology, Mathura is the birthplace of Lord Krishna, a central figure in the epic Mahabharata. The city is mentioned in ancient texts such as the Ramayana, indicating its significance even in pre-historic times. The region around Mathura was known as Braj Bhoomi and was a major center of trade and commerce, facilitated by its strategic location along the Yamuna River.

Buddhist Influence

In the 6th century BCE, Mathura emerged as a vital hub for Buddhism. The city played a significant role in the spread of Buddhist teachings across northern India. Numerous monasteries, stupas, and viharas were constructed during this period, making Mathura a center of Buddhist learning and culture. The famous Greek historian Megasthenes, who visited India in the 4th century BCE, noted the presence of Buddhists in Mathura. The archaeological excavations have unearthed a plethora of Buddhist relics, including statues of Buddha and Bodhisattvas, attesting to the city's Buddhist heritage.

Medieval Period

During the medieval period, Mathura was ruled by a succession of powerful dynasties, including the Mauryas, Kushans, and Guptas. Under the Mauryan Empire, especially during the reign of Emperor Ashoka, Mathura continued to thrive as a religious and cultural center. The Kushan period, particularly under Emperor Kanishka, saw Mathura become a prominent center for art and sculpture. The Mathura School of Art flourished during this time, producing exquisite sculptures and reliefs that depicted Buddhist, Jain, and Hindu themes. The Gupta Empire, known for its golden age of Indian culture, further contributed to Mathura's prosperity and architectural grandeur.

Early Islamic Period

With the decline of the Gupta Empire, Mathura faced several invasions, including those by the Huns. However, it was during the early Islamic period that Mathura experienced significant upheaval. In the 11th century, Mahmud of Ghazni invaded and plundered the city, leading to the destruction of many temples and monuments. Despite this, Mathura managed to retain its religious importance, especially for Hindus who continued to visit the city for its sacred sites.

Mughal Era

The Mughal era marked another chapter of transformation for Mathura. Akbar, the third Mughal emperor, is known for his policy of religious tolerance, which included respect for Mathura's Hindu heritage. He even participated in Hindu festivals and rituals. However, the reign of Aurangzeb in the late 17th century brought renewed challenges. Aurangzeb, known for his orthodox Islamic policies, ordered the destruction of several Hindu temples in Mathura, including the Keshav Dev Temple. Despite these challenges, the city's religious and cultural life continued, and it remained an important pilgrimage site.

Modern Era

In the colonial period, Mathura came under British rule, which brought both challenges and modernization. The British constructed roads, railways, and other infrastructure, making the city more accessible to pilgrims and traders. Post-independence, Mathura has continued to grow as a major urban center. Today, Mathura is a vibrant city that attracts millions of devotees and tourists from around the world. The Krishna Janmabhoomi temple complex is a major attraction, alongside numerous other temples and historical sites. The city's cultural life is enriched by festivals such as Janmashtami, Holi, and Diwali, which are celebrated with great fervor.
